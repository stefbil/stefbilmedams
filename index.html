Abstract

High-fidelity vocal processing is frequently compromised by sibilance, a phenomenon characterized by stochastic high-frequency energy that presents unique dynamic range challenges. While traditional de-essing techniques often rely on static frequency bands, they fail to account for inter-speaker variability and changing dynamics. This paper presents an adaptive real-time de-essing application, developed using the JUCE framework, which automatically detects and suppresses sibilant frequencies. The proposed methodology integrates a derivative-based frequency tracking algorithm to estimate a spectral-centroid-like statistic without the computational overhead of the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). This is coupled with a dual-path envelope detection system and a relative threshold logic to distinguish sibilance from the wideband signal. Additionally, a dynamic harmonic exciter is implemented to restore high-frequency presence during non-sibilant periods. Objective spectral analysis confirms the system's ability to selectively attenuate energy in the 2–11 kHz range while maintaining spectral transparency and minimizing artifacts. Across three vocal files, the adaptive method improves attenuation-frequency alignment relative to static processing by an average of 99.58 Hz, supporting the core contribution of automatic center-frequency tracking under speaker-dependent sibilance variation.

I. Introduction

The engineering of high fidelity vocal processing tools remains one of the most persistent challenges in digital signal processing. Among the various artifacts inherent to vocal recording, sibilance, which is the high-frequency acoustic energy generated by turbulent airflow through the narrow channel of the vocal tract during fricative consonant articulation, poses a unique dynamic range problem. Sibilant sounds, such as /s/, /z/, /ʃ/ (sh), and /tʃ/ (ch), are characterized by stochastic noise-like waveforms [1] with spectral energy typically concentrated between 2 kHz and 11 kHz. These frequencies often exhibit disproportionately high peak amplitudes compared to the vowel components of speech, particularly when subjected to heavy compression or saturation downstream in the signal chain. De-essing is a technique to reduce sibilant sounds in speech signals [2].

Sibilance frequency varies with speaker physiology, microphone placement, and recording environment [1]. Unlike static de-essers, which target a fixed frequency band requiring per-session manual tuning, in this paper, we present an adaptive vocal de-essing processor that automatically detects sibilant audio, suppresses the signal, and dynamically applies excitation when needed, in real-time. Our de-esser is adaptive, i.e., sibilant frequencies are detected automatically [3]. The adaptive approach follows the principle established by Linhard et al. [3], who demonstrated that automatic frequency adaptation reduces audible artifacts compared to fixed-band processing.

For vocal recordings that suffer from excessive sibilance, the challenge is to maintain consistent vocal clarity across changing dynamics and timbres without manual equalization or multiple processors. Sibilance frequency varies systematically with speaker anatomy, recording microphone, and room acoustics [1], meaning a fixed \(f_c\) optimised for one session may underperform on another. This inter-session variability motivates an automatic center-frequency adaptation strategy that requires no manual tuning.

The hypothesis for this project was that since classic de-essing processors often use a static frequency band, an adaptive frequency tracking system is proposed that will dynamically adapt to any vocal input, without the excessive need of manual frequency tuning.

II. Methodology

To test this hypothesis, a real-time audio VST3 plugin was developed using C++ and the JUCE framework. The methodology integrates digital signal processing (DSP) theory regarding state variable filter (SVF) with software engineering patterns for real-time performance [4, 5].

A. Signal Decomposition

The input signal \(x[n]\) is split into two parallel analysis paths:

Broadband path: A copy of the raw input signal, representing the full spectral energy of the signal.

Sidechain path (sibilance isolation): Processed signal through a digital state variable filter configured as a bandpass filter.

Throughout this paper, \(f_s\) denotes the system sample rate in Hz, and \(n\) indexes discrete time samples. The SVF topology was chosen for its numerical stability and the ability to modulate frequency coefficients at audio rates without artifacts [4, 5]. The configuration of the bandpass is:

$$ y_{sib}[n] = H_{\text{BP}}(x[n],\, f_c,\, Q) $$

where \(f_c\) is the center frequency (default at 6 kHz) and \(Q\) controls bandwidth with a default at 2.0. The bandpass output isolates energy in the sibilant region while rejecting low-frequency fundamentals and high-frequency noise.

B. Crossover Network

For the split-band processing, a 4th-order Linkwitz-Riley crossover (24 dB/Octave) decomposes the signal into low and high bands. This is implemented by cascading two 2nd-order SVF stages in series.

The high band signal is derived via complementary subtraction to ensure perfect reconstruction:

$$ x_{low}[n] = H_{\text{LP}}\left(H_{\text{LP}}(x[n],\,f_{split}),\,f_{split}\right) $$
$$ x_{high}[n] = x[n] - x_{low}[n] $$

where \(H_{\text{BP}}\) from (1) denotes the SVF bandpass transfer function and \(H_{\text{LP}}\) denotes the SVF lowpass transfer function, both implemented as juce::dsp::StateVariableTPTFilter.

The complementary-subtraction topology guarantees flat magnitude summation (perfect reconstruction), avoiding the comb filtering that occurs when using independent LP and HP filters [6]. The crossover frequency \(f_{split}\) is user adjustable between 3–10 kHz.

C. Dual Envelope Detection

To establish the relative threshold, the system calculates two control voltages representing the instantaneous loudness of different spectral regions.

Envelope Follower with Asymmetric Time Constants

Let \(u[n]\) denote the input to the envelope follower (either \(x[n]\) for the broadband path or \(y_{sib}[n]\) for the sibilance path), and let \(e[n]\) denote the resulting envelope output.

$$ e[n] = |u[n]| + \alpha[n] \cdot (e[n-1] - |u[n]|) $$

where the coefficient \(\alpha[n]\) switches based on signal direction:

$$ \alpha[n] = \begin{cases} \alpha_{\mathrm{atk}}, & \text{if } |u[n]| > e[n-1] \quad (\text{rising}), \\ \alpha_{\mathrm{rel}}, & \text{if } |u[n]| \le e[n-1] \quad (\text{falling}). \end{cases} $$

The time constants are derived from user-defined attack and release times:

$$ \alpha_{\mathrm{atk}} = \exp\left(\frac{-1}{f_s \cdot t_{\mathrm{atk}}}\right) $$
$$ \alpha_{\mathrm{rel}} = \exp\left(\frac{-1}{f_s \cdot t_{\mathrm{rel}}}\right) $$

where \(t_{\mathrm{atk}}\) and \(t_{\mathrm{rel}}\) are attack/release times in seconds. Fast attack prevents sibilant transients from escaping detection, while slow release avoids chattering on decaying phonemes.

Dual Path Computation

Two envelopes are calculated in parallel:

  • \(E_{wide}[n]\): Envelope of unfiltered input signal \(x[n]\)
  • \(E_{sib}[n]\): Envelope of the bandpass filtered signal \(y_{sib}[n]\)

These are summed across all input channels to create control signals:

$$ E_{\text{wide}}^{\text{total}}[n] = \sum_{ch=1}^{N_{ch}} E_{\text{wide},\,ch}[n] $$
$$ E_{sib}^{\text{total}}[n] = \sum_{ch=1}^{N_{ch}} E_{sib,\,ch}[n] $$

D. Harmonic Exciter

De-essing necessarily attenuates high-frequency energy, which can reduce perceived vocal brightness even during non-sibilant phonemes if spectral spread is wide. To compensate, a dynamic harmonic exciter is activated inversely to the sibilance detector: it engages when the sibilance-to-broadband ratio falls significantly below the detection threshold, i.e. when the vocal is clean and could benefit from added air, rather than when sibilance is present.

The excitation control signal \(C_{excite}[n]\) quantifies how far the current spectral ratio is below threshold. When \(C_{excite}[n] > 0.01\), saturation drive is:

$$ D[n] = 1 + 3 \cdot A_{\text{excite}} \cdot C_{\text{excite}}[n] $$

where \(A_{\text{excite}}\) is the Excite Amount parameter between 0–100%. Soft clipping is defined as:

$$ y_{\text{sat}}[n] = \tanh(D[n] \cdot x_{\text{high}}[n]) $$

Normalization is defined as:

$$ y_{\text{norm}}[n] = \frac{y_{\text{sat}}[n]}{\tanh(D[n])} $$

This preserves gain while introducing odd-harmonic content [7]. Amplitude boost is defined as:

$$ B[n] = 10^{(9 \cdot A_{\text{excite}} \cdot C_{\text{excite}}[n])/20} $$

Wet & Dry mix is defined as:

$$ W = M_{\text{excite}} \cdot C_{\text{excite}}[n] $$
$$ y_{\text{final}}[n] = x_{\text{high}}[n] + W \cdot (y_{\text{norm}}[n] \cdot B[n] - x_{\text{high}}[n]) $$

where \(M_{\text{excite}}\) is the Excite Mix parameter from 0–100%. As a result, non-sibilant consonants and vowel transitions gain subtle harmonic richness without re-introducing harshness.

E. Relative Threshold Detection Logic

The detection decision is made by comparing the sibilant envelope to a threshold-scaled version of the wideband envelope:

if \(E_{sib}[n] > E_{wide}[n] \cdot K_{\text{thresh}}\) then apply suppression.

Where \(K_{\text{thresh}}\) is derived from the user adjustable threshold parameter (in dB):

$$ K_{\text{thresh}} = 10^{T_{dB}/20} $$

Upon detecting sibilance, the gain reduction factor is calculated using a power law compression curve:

$$ \text{OverRatio} = \frac{E_{sib}[n]}{E_{wide}[n] \cdot K_{\text{thresh}} + \epsilon} $$
$$ g[n] = \left(\frac{E_{sib}[n]}{E_{wide}[n] \cdot K_{\text{thresh}}}\right)^{-A} $$

where \(A \in [0,1]\) is the suppression amount, and \(\epsilon = 10^{-9}\) prevents division by zero in ratio operations. This produces a smooth, program-dependent compression characteristic analogous to the ballistic behavior of optical compressors, as described in the gain computer formulations of [5].

Conversely, when sibilance is below threshold (\(E_{sib}[n] < E_{wide}[n] \cdot K_{\text{thresh}}\)), the system calculates an excitation control signal:

$$ \text{Ratio}_{lin} = \frac{E_{sib}[n]}{E_{wide}[n] + \epsilon} $$
$$ \text{Ratio}_{dB} = 20 \log_{10}(\text{Ratio}_{lin}) $$
$$ C_{excite}[n] = \text{clip}\left(\frac{(T_{dB} + 20) - \text{Ratio}_{dB}}{12},\, 0,\, 1\right) $$

This normalized control signal (\(0 \le C_{excite} \le 1\)) drives the harmonic exciter, with higher values indicating more sibilance deficit.

F. Derivative-Based Frequency Estimation

When Auto Freq mode is enabled, the system estimates the dominant sibilance frequency without a DFT. The estimator operates on the high-band signal \(x_{high}[n]\) (post-crossover) to suppress influence from low-frequency fundamentals.

Two finite difference operators approximate signal velocity and acceleration:

$$ d_1[n] = x_{high}[n] - x_{high}[n-1] $$
$$ d_2[n] = d_1[n] - d_1[n-1] $$

The ratio of acceleration energy to velocity energy over a block of \(N\) samples yields an instantaneous angular frequency estimate:

$$ \hat{\omega}[k] = \sqrt{\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} d_2^2[n]}{\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} d_1^2[n]}} $$

Converting to Hz:

$$ \hat{f}[k] = \frac{f_s}{2\pi}\,\hat{\omega}[k] $$

This estimator is equivalent to a zero-crossing rate computed on the derivative signal, and converges to the spectral centroid for narrowband signals [4]. The estimate is clamped to the sibilance range \([3\,\text{kHz},\, 11\,\text{kHz}]\) and smoothed with a first-order IIR (time constant \(0.1\,\text{s}\)) to prevent rapid modulation of the SVF center frequency:

$$ f_c[k] = f_c[k-1] + \Delta_{\max} \cdot \text{clip}\left(\frac{\hat{f}[k] - f_c[k-1]}{\Delta_{\max}},\, -1,\, 1\right) $$

where \(\Delta_{\max} = 25\,\text{Hz/block}\) is the slew limit that prevents erratic SVF center-frequency modulation caused by high-frequency noise amplification inherent in differentiation.

G. Lookahead Delay Compensation

To allow the envelope follower to observe transients before they reach the processing stage, a 2 ms circular delay buffer is implemented. This ensures the attack phase of compression is without any artifacts, as the system knows about transients 2 ms in advance [4].

III. Implementation

A. Framework Architecture

The application is delivered as a VST3 plugin and a standalone executable, both built from a single juce::AudioProcessor subclass.

B. Audio Processing Thread

The processBlock() method receives audio buffers from the DAW host or any microphone input.

Parameter Management

User controls are managed via APVTS, i.e., AudioProcessorValueTreeState. Parameters are thread safe atomics, readable from the audio thread without locks.

C. Visualization System

Two separate visualization modules run on a refresh rate of 60 Hz on the message thread. Waveform Scope displays processed audio with suppression/excitation overlays. It utilizes a lock-free First In First Out (FIFO) to transfer data from the audio thread.

The FFT Spectrum Analyzer displays frequency content and detector filter response. It uses JUCE's dsp::FFT class with Blackman-Harris windowing of 2048 points and 512 bin outputs [8]. The FFT is computed only for the graphics user interface (GUI) display and is not used in the audio processing chain.

IV. Results and Analysis

A. Methodology

To objectively validate the system's frequency selectivity and transient response, a comparative spectral analysis was conducted on a vocal recording containing pronounced sibilant fricatives (/s/, /z/). The primary objective was to verify that the adaptive algorithm could successfully isolate and attenuate sibilant energy in the time-frequency domain without degrading the fundamental vocal signal.

The test utilized a male vocal recording, produced by ElevenLabs Text-To-Speech v3 [9] Generative-AI model, characterized by harsh sibilance in the 6 kHz–11 kHz range. The plugin was configured in Auto-Freq mode with the Split-Band mode active. The threshold was set to −20 dB with a ratio of 25%, while the envelope ballistics were tuned to a fast attack (2 ms) and moderate release (80 ms) to match the transient nature of fricative consonants.

B. Spectral Comparison

A direct comparison of the dry signal and the signal through the processing algorithm reveals a distinct reduction in energy density within the identified sibilant bandwidth. Crucially, the spectral energy below 4 kHz remains visually stable, demonstrating high spectral transparency.

The upper pane displays the amplitude envelopes of the source (blue) and processed (orange) signals, together with the excitation control signal \(C_{excite}[n]\) (brown). The envelopes are tightly aligned during non-sibilant periods, confirming that the processor behaves as a near-unity-gain system when suppression is inactive. The lower pane isolates the instantaneous gain reduction \(\Delta G[n] = 1 - g[n]\), where \(g[n]\) is defined in the gain reduction equation. The calculated metrics for this performance window reveal a maximum gain reduction of 19.75 dB occurring at the most aggressive sibilant peak (\(t \approx 17.5\text{s}\)). Conversely, the average gain reduction is merely 0.67 dB across the entire sample. This low average value quantitatively demonstrates transparency. Strong attenuation is applied only during brief sibilant events, leaving the majority of the vocal performance statistically unaltered.

The combined data supports the validation of adaptive tracking and dynamic processing. The spectrograms are consistent with the derivative-based estimator and its slew-constrained update, while gain reduction statistics demonstrate transparency during non-sibilant intervals. The variability in reduction depth indicates that attenuation scales with instantaneous sibilance-to-wideband contrast as defined by the gain reduction equation.

C. Comparison Against Static De-Esser Baseline

To evaluate the practical effect of adaptive frequency tracking, the proposed system was compared against the Renaissance DeEsser from Waves Audio. The baseline is a commercially available plugin. The detection is fixed at \(f_c = 7.2\,\text{kHz}\).

Both conditions were applied to the same TTS vocal excerpt under matched settings: threshold \(= -20\,\text{dB}\), suppression amount \(= 25\%\), attack \(= 2\,\text{ms}\), and release \(= 80\,\text{ms}\).

Two complementary evaluation groups were computed offline in Python (librosa, scipy): (i) suppression metrics (sibilance-band RMS reduction over 4–11 kHz, broadband RMS error over 20 Hz–4 kHz, and over-suppression rate), and (ii) frequency-tracking alignment metrics, defined by frame-wise mean absolute error (MAE) between the dry sibilance peak frequency and the attenuation-frequency centroid applied by each method.

Table I: Objective comparison between Renaissance DeEsser (static) and the proposed adaptive algorithm.
Condition Sib. RMS Reduction (dB) Broadband Err. (dB) Over-suppress (%)
Static (\(f_c = 7.2\,\text{kHz}\)) 6.89 0.20 29.28
Adaptive (proposed) 2.94 0.67 10.97

Relative to the static baseline, the adaptive condition reduces sibilance-band attenuation by 3.95 dB and increases broadband deviation by 0.47 dB, while reducing over-suppression by 18.31 percentage points. Critically, the static baseline's stronger raw attenuation reflects manual tuning of \(f_c = 7.2\,\text{kHz}\) to the test file's known sibilance peak, a condition that presupposes knowledge of the speaker's sibilance profile in advance. The adaptive method's contribution lies not in attenuation depth under manually matched conditions, but in frequency-tracking accuracy across sessions where such tuning is unavailable, as evidenced by the tracking alignment table below.

Table II: Frequency-tracking alignment across three files (lower MAE is better).
File Set Adaptive MAE (Hz) Static MAE (Hz) Advantage (Hz)
vocal_1 2021.45 2132.82 +111.37
vocal_2 1187.44 1358.86 +171.42
vocal_3 1148.99 1164.94 +15.95
Mean 1452.63 1552.21 +99.58

The tracking results show consistent alignment improvement for adaptive processing across all three tested files. The absolute MAE values (1148–2021 Hz) reflect the coarse temporal resolution of the block-wise derivative estimator and the inherently broadband spectral character of sibilant fricatives, rather than a narrow-band tracking failure. The consistent directional improvement over static processing confirms that automatic center-frequency adaptation reduces alignment error even under adverse conditions. Therefore, even when a static setting can produce stronger raw attenuation under manually matched conditions, the adaptive method better follows time-varying sibilance location which is the primary objective of the proposed algorithm.

V. Discussion

A. Limitations

While the proposed system demonstrates robust performance across diverse vocal material, several areas warrant further investigation.

The derivative-based approach exhibits reduced accuracy under specific conditions:

High Noise Floors: Differentiation amplifies high-frequency noise at +6 dB/octave per derivative order. When the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) falls below 30 dB in the 6–12 kHz band, spurious frequency estimates occur. This manifests as an erratic modulation of the SVF center frequency driven by spurious low-frequency energy leaking into the derivative calculation. Because differentiation amplifies spectral content at \(+6\,\text{dB/octave}\) per order, low-frequency noise, though low in absolute amplitude, can dominate the ratio in the angular frequency estimate equation and produce frequency estimates far outside the sibilance band. The slew limiter mitigates rapid jumps, and the crossover high-band itself serves as the pre-emphasis stage, attenuating sub-\(f_{split}\) content before differentiation, reducing false estimates at low SNR.

B. Future Work

Multiband Excitation: The current excitation module operates exclusively on the high band. Human hearing exhibits frequency-dependent saturation preferences: even-harmonic distortion enhances perceived warmth in the 2–4 kHz "presence" region, while odd-harmonic content contributes brightness above 8 kHz [10]. A multi-band architecture could apply tailored saturation curves across three frequency zones; low: 80–500 Hz, mid: 500–4000 Hz, high: 4000–16000 Hz.

Machine Learning-Based Phoneme Classification: The derivative-based frequency tracker makes no distinction between sibilant phonemes (/s/, /z/, /sh/, /zh/) and non-sibilant high-frequency content (breath noise, tongue clicks, lip smacks). Machine learning approaches, such as convolutional neural networks trained on phonetically annotated speech corpora, could potentially classify audio frames as sibilant vs. non-sibilant, reducing false positives. However, such approaches must maintain real-time inference latency below 5 ms to preserve system responsiveness.

Adaptive Feedback Suppression: Live sound systems suffer from feedback when microphone-captured sound re-enters the system via loudspeakers, typically as narrow, high-Q resonances. Automatic feedback suppression systems using adaptive notch filters [11] could potentially benefit from derivative-based frequency tracking approaches, though the faster response times required for feedback control present additional implementation challenges.

Speech Enhancement for Telecommunications: VoIP systems degrade intelligibility under low-bitrate compression and packet loss. While traditional speech enhancement focuses on noise reduction [12], harmonic enhancement techniques could provide an alternative or complementary approach for improving perceived clarity in bandwidth limited channels.
